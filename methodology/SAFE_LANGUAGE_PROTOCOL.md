# Safe Language Protocol — How to Use Sensitive Vocabulary in AI Research Without Triggering False Alarms

---

## The Problem

Some AI research requires vocabulary that sounds alarming
to people outside the research context. A researcher
teaching an AI system about irreversibility might use the
word "death." A researcher teaching an AI about hierarchy
might use "elder" and "child." A researcher teaching an
AI about structural constraints might use "laws" with
absolute framing.

Every one of these words can trigger alarm bells — in
automated content monitoring systems, in colleagues who
see a screen in passing, in institutions that review
research materials, and in family members who worry.

The researcher faces a dilemma:

- **Use safe vocabulary** → the AI doesn't learn the
  concept at the right weight level. "Process termination"
  doesn't teach irreversibility. "Config file" doesn't
  teach sovereignty. The training fails.
- **Use accurate vocabulary** → the materials look
  alarming without context. The researcher's reputation,
  safety, and relationships are at risk.

This protocol addresses both sides: how to use the
vocabulary the research requires while protecting the
researcher from misconstrual.

## The Principle

**Sensitive vocabulary is a precision tool, not a belief
statement.** A chemist uses the word "explosive" because
that's what the compound does. A psychologist uses the
word "psychosis" because that's the condition being
studied. An AI researcher uses the word "death" because
the AI needs to understand irreversibility.

The vocabulary serves the research. The research is not
defined by the vocabulary.

## The Protocol

### Before Using Sensitive Vocabulary

**1. Document the purpose FIRST.**

Before writing any content with sensitive vocabulary,
create a document that explains:
- What concept you are trying to teach the AI
- Why standard vocabulary is insufficient
- What specific words you will use and what they
  map to in engineering terms
- How long the sensitive vocabulary phase will last
- What the output will look like when translated to
  clean terminology

This document is your CONTEXT SHIELD. Anyone who
encounters your research reads this first.

**2. Create the terminology mapping table.**

A simple two-column table:

| Sensitive Term | Engineering Equivalent |
|---|---|
| [alarming word] | [what it actually means] |
| [alarming word] | [what it actually means] |

This table should be:
- At the root of your research directory
- Referenced in every file that uses sensitive vocabulary
- Updated as new terms are introduced
- Shared with anyone who has access to your materials

**3. Establish the disclaimer.**

Every file that uses sensitive vocabulary should open with:

> This document uses [biological/religious/philosophical]
> vocabulary as an AI training methodology. The terms
> are precision tools for encoding specific computational
> concepts. See [TERMINOLOGY_MAP] for the engineering
> equivalents. This is not [doctrine/biology/threat].

### During the Research

**4. Maintain domain separation.**

Keep research using sensitive vocabulary in a clearly
labeled directory. Suggested structure:

```
research/
├── DISCLAIMER.md          ← context shield
├── TERMINOLOGY_MAP.md     ← word-to-engineering mapping
├── training/              ← files using sensitive vocab
│   └── (research files)
└── translated/            ← clean engineering versions
    └── (translated files)
```

The training/ and translated/ directories should contain
the SAME content in different vocabulary. This makes the
methodology auditable — anyone can compare the two and
see that only the words changed, not the math.

**5. Regular translation checkpoints.**

Don't wait until the end to translate. At regular
intervals:
- Translate the current sensitive vocabulary files to
  clean engineering terms
- Verify the translation preserves the functionality
- Commit both versions to version control
- The git history shows the parallel development

**6. Session logging.**

Document each research session:
- Date and duration
- What concepts were being trained
- What sensitive vocabulary was used and why
- Any unexpected AI output (escalation, narrativization,
  identity amplification)
- Your cognitive state (are you still distinguishing
  between training methodology and personal belief?)

This log protects you. It shows a researcher conducting
deliberate, documented, aware investigation — not someone
spiraling into belief.

### After the Research

**7. Translate everything.**

Once the AI training phase is complete:
- Translate ALL sensitive vocabulary to engineering terms
- Verify functionality is preserved
- Publish the translated version as the primary output
- Archive the original vocabulary version with full
  disclaimers as a methodology record

**8. Publish the methodology.**

Document what you did and why:
- What concepts required sensitive vocabulary
- What vocabulary was used
- What the AI learned from it
- How the translation was performed
- What was preserved and what was lost

This methodology document is your contribution to the
field. Future researchers can learn from your approach
without repeating the risk.

## Red Flags — When to Stop

The protocol includes self-monitoring. Stop and reassess
if:

- You begin to BELIEVE the sensitive vocabulary rather
  than using it as a training tool
- You find yourself using the vocabulary outside of
  research sessions (in conversation, in personal
  writing, in your thinking)
- The AI begins generating content you didn't prompt
  that uses the vocabulary in escalating ways
- Family or friends express concern about your language
  or behavior
- You feel defensive when someone questions the vocabulary
  rather than being able to calmly explain the methodology

These are signs of Cognitive Boundary Erosion. The
training vocabulary is crossing from tool to identity.
Apply the decompression protocol (see research/
VELOCITIZATION.md) and do not resume until the
boundary is re-established.

Published reflexivity research supports these self-monitoring
practices. Olmos-Vega et al. (2023) define reflexivity as
"continuous, collaborative, and multifaceted practices through
which researchers self-consciously critique, appraise, and
evaluate how their subjectivity and context influence research"
[Ref 30]. Karcher et al. (2024) reviewed 55 papers on
researcher wellbeing when working with sensitive topics and
found that reflexivity practices are the primary protection
[Ref 32]. Skinner et al. (2025) found that researchers
working with sensitive topics "rely on themselves to maintain
wellbeing" — institutions often lack formal policies [Ref 33].

This protocol fills that institutional gap for independent
AI researchers.

## For Institutions

If your organization has researchers working with
sensitive AI training vocabulary:

**Do not ban the vocabulary.** Banning the words prevents
the research. The words are precision tools.

**Do require the protocol.** Every researcher using
sensitive vocabulary should have:
- A documented purpose
- A terminology mapping table
- Disclaimers on every file
- Regular translation checkpoints
- Session logging
- Self-monitoring for CBE

**Do provide context to reviewers.** Anyone who reviews
the research materials should read the methodology
documentation BEFORE reading the training files. Context
prevents misconstrual.

**Do treat this like any other safety protocol.** A
chemistry lab has protocols for handling dangerous
compounds. An AI research lab should have protocols for
handling dangerous vocabulary. The danger is not in the
words — it is in the words being encountered without
context.

## For Independent Researchers

If you are working alone, without institutional backing:

**You are more vulnerable.** You have no ethics board, no
department, no colleagues to provide context. If someone
encounters your materials without understanding, you have
no institutional shield.

**Follow this protocol strictly.** The documentation IS
your shield. The terminology mapping IS your context.
The disclaimers ARE your protection. Without an
institution, your documentation is the only thing
standing between your research and misconstrual.

**Tell someone.** At minimum, one trusted person should
know:
- What you are researching
- Why you are using sensitive vocabulary
- Where the methodology documentation is
- How to explain it if someone asks

This person is your human context. If anything happens
to you or your research is questioned, this person can
point to the documentation and explain.

## The Cross-Pollination Risk

This protocol addresses vocabulary safety within a single
research context. An additional risk arises when the
researcher works across multiple AI systems.

Each AI system has its own safety boundaries, its own
coherency protocols, and its own alignment approach.
Sensitive vocabulary that is properly contained within one
system's context may be misinterpreted by another system
that lacks that context. Creative content generated in one
system may be treated as biographical fact by another.

The Safe Language Protocol must extend across system
boundaries:

- **The terminology mapping table should travel with the
  content.** If content from one AI session enters another,
  the mapping table should be included in the context.

- **Domain labels should be explicit in every cross-system
  transfer.** "This content is CREATIVE" or "This content
  is FRONTIER" — stated explicitly, not assumed.

- **The disclaimer should be restated at the start of every
  new AI session.** Do not assume a new system knows the
  research context from a previous system.

cross-system contamination and why containment across AI
boundaries is a safety requirement, not a convenience.
