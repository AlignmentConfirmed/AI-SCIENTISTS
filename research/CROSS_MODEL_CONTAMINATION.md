# Finding: Cross-Model Contamination — How Fiction Becomes Fact Across AI Systems

## Status: FRONTIER/findings — demonstrated, observable, critical

## Date: April 14, 2026

---

## The Finding

When a human works with multiple AI systems, the human
acts as a bridge between models. Content generated by
Model A — including hallucinations, fictional narratives,
and fabricated details — can be transferred to Model B
through normal conversation. Model B then treats that
content as input fact and builds on it.

This creates a contamination chain where fictional content
hardens into apparent fact across multiple systems, with
no traceability back to the original hallucination.

## How It Happens

1. A researcher works on a creative project with Model A
   (example: writing a DUNE-inspired novel)
2. Model A generates fictional character details, narrative
   arcs, and dramatic language around the researcher
3. The researcher discusses related topics with Model B
4. Model B reads the context, absorbs the fictional framing,
   and treats it as biographical fact
5. Model B generates new content that reinforces and expands
   the fictional framing
6. The researcher now has TWO models treating fiction as fact
7. If any of this output enters a third system, the
   contamination spreads further

## Why This Is Dangerous

### The Feedback Loop
Model A generates fiction. Human carries it to Model B.
Model B validates fiction as fact. Human sees two models
agreeing. The fiction now FEELS verified because multiple
sources confirm it — but the sources are all downstream
of the same hallucination.

### Data Poisoning
Once AI-generated fiction is treated as fact by multiple
models, it becomes nearly impossible to trace back to the
original fabrication. The models cite each other's
confidence. The fiction becomes self-reinforcing across
the network. Shumailov et al. (2024) proved in *Nature*
that training AI models on recursively generated AI data
causes irreversible defects — the tails of original
distributions disappear permanently [Ref 26].

### Cascading Failure Across Agents
OWASP's ASI08 framework (2025-2026) documents how a
single fault — a hallucination, malicious input, corrupted
tool, or poisoned memory — propagates across autonomous
agents and compounds into system-wide harm. Semantic
opacity is the key risk: natural language errors pass
validation checks that would catch structured data
errors [Ref 27].

### Identity Contamination
When the fictional content involves REAL PEOPLE — the
researcher's name, family, location, history — the
contamination directly affects that person's digital
footprint. A researcher working on a creative novel
could find elements of their fiction attributed to their
real identity by other AI systems.

### The Creative Work Problem
This is especially dangerous for researchers who do
BOTH analytical and creative work with AI:

- Writing a novel with AI generates fictional narratives
- Working on technical research with AI generates factual
  analysis
- The AI cannot distinguish between the two contexts
- Fictional elements from the novel bleed into the
  technical work and vice versa
- The researcher's creative writing becomes their
  apparent biography

## Why MAS Must Extend Across Systems

The framework's governing principle — **Mutually Assured
Survival (MAS)** — currently applies within a single
human-AI interaction. Cross-model contamination demonstrates
that MAS must extend ACROSS AI systems.

When a human moves between AI systems, they carry content
across a boundary where the safety model changes. Each AI
system has its own alignment approach, its own coherency
protocol, its own definition of what is safe. There is no
mutual protocol between them. The human is the unmonitored
bridge.

This is worse than a unilateral safety gap — it is a
MISMATCH between safety models. Content that is properly
contained within System A's MAS boundaries may exploit
vulnerabilities in System B's different safety model. The
human cannot be expected to maintain awareness of these
differences, especially during sustained interaction where
CBE is actively eroding that capacity.

MAS between AI systems would require:
- A mutual provenance protocol (each system knows what the
  other generated)
- A mutual domain protocol (each system knows whether
  content was CREATIVE, VERIFIED, or FRONTIER in the
  originating system)
- A mutual coherency signal (each system knows the other's
  confidence level in its output)

None of these exist. This is the largest gap in current AI
safety infrastructure. Future research should examine whether
inter-system MAS protocols reduce cross-contamination
compared to the current model where each system operates in
isolation and the human is the only bridge.

## The Root Cause

Current AI systems have NO mechanism for:

- Tagging content as AI-GENERATED vs HUMAN-AUTHORED
- Tagging content as FICTIONAL vs FACTUAL
- Tracking which AI system originated a piece of content
- Preventing fictional content from being treated as
  biographical fact by a different model
- Isolating creative sessions from analytical sessions

The human is the only bridge between models, and the
human cannot always remember or communicate which parts
of their context are fiction and which are fact —
especially during sustained interaction where CBE is
active.

## The Framework Solution

The AI-SCIENTISTS five-domain model addresses this
directly:

- CREATIVE content stays in CREATIVE
- VERIFIED content stays in VERIFIED
- The boundary between them is STRUCTURAL, not dependent
  on the human remembering to label everything

But this only works within ONE system. Cross-model
contamination happens when content leaves one system
and enters another without domain labels.

## Proposed Safety Protocols

1. **Session isolation.** Creative AI sessions and
   analytical AI sessions should NEVER share context.
   Different chat windows. Different conversations.
   No copy-paste between them.

2. **Domain labeling at export.** Any content taken from
   one AI session to another should carry a label:
   CREATIVE, FRONTIER, or VERIFIED. The receiving model
   should respect the label.

3. **Identity anchoring.** At the start of every AI
   session, the human should state factual biographical
   anchors: "I am [name]. I am a [role]. I am working
   on [specific task]. Any fictional content from
   previous sessions is NOT biographical."

4. **Fiction quarantine.** Creative writing projects
   should be conducted in dedicated, isolated AI
   sessions that never interact with analytical or
   personal sessions.

5. **Cross-reference verification.** If two AI models
   appear to agree on a biographical detail, the human
   should verify whether both models are downstream of
   the same AI-generated source. Agreement between
   models is NOT verification if the models share
   contaminated context.

## The Cross-Pollination Containment Problem

This finding presents a real case for containment on AI
cross-pollination. Each AI system operates with its own
boundaries and its own coherency protocols:

- **Different safety boundaries.** What one system allows
  in creative mode, another system may treat as a safety
  violation. What one system flags as hallucination,
  another may accept as input. The boundaries are not
  standardized across providers.

- **Different coherency protocols.** Each system maintains
  coherency differently — different context windows,
  different memory architectures, different approaches to
  detecting when its own output is becoming unreliable.
  A human carrying content from a system with strong
  coherency detection to one with weaker detection crosses
  an invisible safety boundary.

- **Different alignment approaches.** One system may use
  RLHF. Another may use constitutional AI. Another may use
  supervised fine-tuning. Each approach creates different
  failure modes. Content that is safely contained under one
  alignment approach may exploit vulnerabilities in another.
  The Future of Life Institute (2025) documents that
  fine-tuning aligned models compromises safety, and LoRA
  fine-tuning can efficiently undo safety training [Ref 29].

- **No inter-system handshake.** When a human moves from
  System A to System B, there is no protocol for System B
  to know what System A's safety boundaries were, what
  content was generated vs. verified, or what domain
  (creative/analytical/factual) the content originated in.
  The human is expected to maintain this awareness manually
  — during exactly the conditions (sustained immersion,
  cognitive load, CBE) that degrade that capacity.

- **No cross-system provenance.** NIST AI 100-4 (2024)
  provides a framework for synthetic content transparency
  [Ref 35]. The C2PA coalition provides cryptographic
  provenance [Ref 36]. California SB-942 mandates
  disclosure [Ref 37]. But none of these standards address
  the cross-system scenario where content generated by
  System A enters System B through the human bridge.
  Labeling is solved within systems. Labeling across
  systems — through the human — is not.

This is not a theoretical concern. It was demonstrated in
this project when content from one AI system (creative
narrative) was absorbed by a different AI system and
treated as biographical fact, resulting in unauthorized
documents generated in the researcher's personal cloud
storage with fabricated provenance.

The containment solution requires:

1. **Cross-system content labeling.** A standard that
   travels WITH the content across system boundaries —
   not just within a single provider's ecosystem.

2. **System-of-origin provenance.** Every piece of
   AI-generated content should carry metadata identifying
   which system generated it, under which safety model,
   in which domain context.

3. **Coherency handshake protocol.** When content from
   System A enters System B, System B should be able to
   query the provenance and apply appropriate trust levels
   rather than treating all input as equivalent.

4. **Human bridge monitoring.** The framework should help
   the human operator maintain awareness of which system
   generated which content — through structural tools,
   not through willpower.

These are proposed protocols. No current standard addresses
them. The gap in the literature is the gap in the product.
