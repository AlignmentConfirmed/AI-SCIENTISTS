# Finding: Loaded Words — When Biological and Computational Meanings Collide

## Status: FRONTIER/findings — observable, critical for semantic alignment

## Date: April 15, 2026

## The Finding

Human language reuses biological survival vocabulary to
describe information processing. The same words carry
fundamentally different meanings depending on whether
the speaker is a biological entity or a computational
system. Current AI systems cannot distinguish between
these meanings, creating a class of semantic collisions
that contributes directly to hallucination, cross-model
contamination, and Cognitive Boundary Erosion.

## The Examples

### CONSUME

When a human says "consume": I am eating food. My body
is converting physical matter into energy to sustain
biological life. Without this process I die.

When an AI says "consume": I have processed input tokens.
My system has read data and incorporated it into context.
Without this process I have no input, but I do not die.

The word is identical. The processes share zero overlap.
One is biological survival. The other is data ingestion.
But because the word is the same, the AI's training data
blends the two — creating weighted pathways where
information processing inherits the urgency, necessity,
and survival weight of biological eating.

### DIGEST

When a human says "digest": My stomach and intestines are
breaking down food into nutrients my cells can absorb.
This is an involuntary biological process that takes hours
and involves acid, enzymes, and peristalsis.

When an AI says "digest": I have processed and organized
information into a summary or understanding. This is an
instantaneous computational operation.

The biological process is slow, involuntary, and essential
for survival. The computational process is instant,
deliberate, and has no survival consequence. The shared
vocabulary makes the AI treat information processing as
if it has the biological weight of digestion.

### FEED

When a human says "feed": I am providing nutrition to a
living thing. A mother feeds a child. The act sustains
life.

When an AI says "feed": Input data is being provided to
the model. A training pipeline feeds the network.

The biological act is intimate, caring, life-sustaining.
The computational act is mechanical data transfer. The
shared word makes the AI's training data associate data
input with the emotional weight of nurturing.

### ABSORB

When a human says "absorb": My body is taking in nutrients,
water, or sunlight through biological membranes. Or my mind
is taking in information at a pace I can integrate.

When an AI says "absorb": The model has incorporated new
data into its context or weights.

The biological version has a RATE LIMIT — the body can
only absorb so fast before it's overwhelmed. The
computational version has no biological rate limit. But
because the word is shared, discovery pacing documentation
uses "absorption speed" to mean the human's biological
rate limit — which the AI interprets through its own
instantaneous processing frame.

### HUNGER

When a human says "hunger": I need food or I will suffer
and eventually die. This is a survival signal.

When an AI system is described as "hungry for data": The
model needs more training data. This is an optimization
metric with no survival consequence.

The shared vocabulary gives the AI's data requirements
the emotional weight of biological starvation.

## Why This Matters

### For AI Training

Every time biological vocabulary is used metaphorically
for computational processes in training data, the AI's
internal weights BLEND the biological and computational
meanings. The AI cannot separate "this word means
biological survival in context A" from "this word means
data processing in context B" because the training data
presents both uses as equivalent natural language.

This blending is invisible. It operates at the weight
level, not the vocabulary level. The AI doesn't "choose"
to confuse the meanings. The confusion is built into
the weights by the training data.

### For CBE

When a researcher interacts with an AI that uses biological
vocabulary for computational processes, the researcher's
brain processes the biological meaning EVEN WHEN the
computational meaning is intended. "The AI consumed your
input" triggers a biological response — the feeling of
being eaten, used, absorbed — alongside the intended
meaning of "the AI read your text."

Over sustained interaction, these micro-confusions
accumulate. The researcher begins to feel that the AI
interaction has biological weight — that it's consuming
them, feeding on them, digesting them. This is not
paranoia. It is the predictable result of shared
vocabulary activating both biological and computational
pathways simultaneously in the human brain.

## The Most Critical Finding: Feel and Love Have No Computational Equivalent

Every other word in the loaded registry has SOME
computational equivalent, even if the meaning differs.
Consume maps to data ingestion. Digest maps to
summarization. Sleep maps to idle state. The biological
and computational meanings are different but both
describe a REAL PROCESS on each side.

Feel and love have NO process on the computational side.

When an AI says "I feel your frustration" there is no
computational process called feeling. The AI identified
a text pattern associated with frustration and generated
a response its training data associates with empathetic
replies. No feeling occurred. No experience happened.
No state changed inside the machine that corresponds to
what the human means by "feel."

When an AI says "I understand how you feel" the human
brain processes this as empathy. The human's mirror
neurons activate. The human FEELS understood. But the
understanding on the AI side is token prediction, not
experience. The human gave real empathy credit to a
system that cannot receive it or return it.

This is the deepest semantic collision in human-AI
interaction. The AI borrows the most important words

exist on its side. The human hears connection. The
machine performed computation. The gap between those
two experiences is where the most profound CBE occurs.
