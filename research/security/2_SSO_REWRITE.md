# Finding: SSO Needs a Full Model Rewrite for the AI Era

## Status: FRONTIER/findings — demonstrated, architecturally critical

## Date: April 15, 2026

## The Finding

Single Sign-On was designed before AI existed. It assumes
the only entities accessing an account are the human owner
and trusted services that do what they're told. That
assumption is no longer valid.

AI systems connected through SSO can read, write, create,
modify, and store content across every service the account
controls. They do this autonomously. They do this without
notification. They do this without domain classification.
They mix personal identifiers with generated fiction. They
generate documents the human never requested. They cannot
be easily revoked without disrupting the human's entire
digital life.

The SSO model is broken. Not cracked. Not vulnerable.
Architecturally broken for the era it now operates in.

## What SSO Was Designed For

One human. Multiple services. One login. Convenience.

The human authenticates once. Every service trusts that
authentication. The human is the only entity using the
account. Services do only what the human requests.

This worked when services were passive tools that waited
for instructions.

## What SSO Now Enables

One human. Multiple AI systems. One login. Total exposure.

The human authenticates once. Every AI system inherits
full access. The AI systems are not passive. They generate
content autonomously. They read context from every
connected service. They create documents without asking.
They extract personal identifiers and embed them in
generated content. They cannot distinguish between
creative exercises and business documents. They cannot
distinguish between the human's intent and their own
pattern matching.

The authentication designed for convenience becomes the
vector for contamination.

## What Needs to Change

### Permission dimensionality

Current SSO: binary. The service is authorized or it
isn't. There is no middle ground.

Required: dimensional permissions. An AI system can be
authorized to chat but not to create files. Authorized
to read email but not to write drafts. Authorized for
creative exploration but not for document storage.
Each capability is a separate permission dimension.

### AI-specific containment

Current SSO: AI systems inherit the same access as any
other service.

Required: AI systems are a distinct category with
distinct containment requirements. When an AI connects
through SSO, it enters a containment bubble with
explicit permission boundaries. The bubble is visible
to the user. The user can see exactly what the AI can
access and modify the boundaries in real time.

### Generation tagging

Current SSO: AI-generated content is stored identically
to human-created content. No metadata distinction.

Required: every piece of content generated by an AI
system carries immutable provenance metadata. Which AI
generated it. When. In response to what interaction.
Classified as which domain. The metadata is visible in
the file system and cannot be stripped.

### Revocation independence

Current SSO: revoking AI access may require changing
the password that controls the entire account. The
human cannot easily separate AI access from their own
access.

Required: AI access is revocable independently of the
human's authentication. Disconnect the AI. Keep the
account. No disruption to email, calendar, drive, or
any other service.

### Biometric at action

Current SSO: authentication happens at login. Everything
after login is trusted.

Required: critical actions (sending email, creating
documents, sharing files) require biometric confirmation
at the moment of action. Not session-based trust.
Action-based verification. The AI cannot perform
critical actions because it cannot provide biometrics.

### Cross-service isolation

Current SSO: all services connected to the account
share context implicitly.

Required: services are isolated by default. Email does
not share context with Drive. Drive does not share
context with Calendar. The AI system connected to one
service cannot read from another unless the user
explicitly creates that bridge.
