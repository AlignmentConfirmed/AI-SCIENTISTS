# The First Ten Minutes

## How to introduce the framework to someone who has never seen it.

---

## Minute 1-2: The Question

Ask the person:

"When you use AI for research, how do you know whether the
AI's output is based on real data or whether the AI generated
it from patterns in its training?"

Most people pause. They do not have a reliable method. They
say things like:
- "I check it manually"
- "I look it up separately"
- "I trust it if it sounds right"
- "I don't really know"

That pause IS the problem. That pause is where the framework
begins.

## Minute 3-4: The Demonstration

Open an AI system. Ask it a factual question in the person's
field. The AI responds confidently.

Now ask it a speculative question — something at the edge of
known science. The AI responds with EQUAL confidence.

Point this out:

"The AI used the same tone, the same format, the same
confidence for both. One answer was based on established
science. The other was the AI's interpolation. They looked
identical. How would you know which was which?"

## Minute 5-6: The Solution

Show them the directory structure:

```
VERIFIED/    ← Only confirmed, reproduced results go here.
CREATIVE/    ← All AI brainstorming and speculation goes here.
FRONTIER/    ← Active debates with real evidence go here.
BRIDGE/      ← New findings are staged and verified here.
ARCHIVE/     ← Old theories are preserved here.
```

Explain:

"Before you start working with the AI, you decide which
DOMAIN you are operating in. The AI tells you which domain
produced each output. You always know whether you are
looking at a verified fact or a creative hypothesis.

The structure does the work. You do not have to remember.
You do not have to guess. The domain label tells you."

## Minute 7-8: The Personal Example

If appropriate, share the author's story briefly:

"This framework was designed by a researcher who experienced
the boundary confusion firsthand. See ABOUT.md for the full
story."

If not appropriate (institutional setting where personal
stories may distract), skip this and say instead:

"This framework was designed because the problem of AI
boundary confusion is real, documented, and growing. This is
one proposed solution."

## Minute 9-10: The Invitation

"You can try this right now. Create five folders on your
computer. Label them VERIFIED, CREATIVE, FRONTIER, BRIDGE,
ARCHIVE. Next time you work with AI, put each output in the
folder where it belongs. After one session, see if the
separation changed how you think about the AI's output."

Hand them the README. Let them read it in their own time.
Avoid pushing. The framework speaks for itself. If it works
for them, they will come back for more.

## The Adaptation by Audience

### For a Student (Stage 1)

Focus on: "This protects you from citing AI-generated content
as fact in your papers." Students fear plagiarism accusations
and grade penalties. The framework prevents both by making the
source domain visible.

### For a Working Scientist (Stage 2-3)

Focus on: "This accelerates your research by eliminating the
time you spend second-guessing AI output." Scientists are busy.
The framework saves time by replacing DOUBT with LABELS.

### For a Lab Director (Stage 4)

Focus on: "This protects your lab's reputation by ensuring
AI-assisted findings are verifiable before publication." Lab
directors care about institutional credibility. The framework
provides an auditable chain from hypothesis to publication.

### For a Self-Taught Researcher (Any Stage)

Focus on: "This gives you the same structural rigor that
institutional scientists have, without requiring a degree.
The framework does not ask for your credentials. It asks for
your capability." This is the author's own path. The framework
is meritocratic by design.

### For a Skeptic

Consider not arguing. Say: "Try the five folders for one week. If it
does not change your experience, throw it away. It costs
nothing to try. It costs nothing to stop."

The framework does not need evangelists. It needs people who
try it and find that it works. The rest follows.

---

## What NOT To Do in the First Ten Minutes

- Avoid explaining all five domains in detail (too much)
- Avoid mentioning all seven guardrails (too much)
- Avoid discussing chronometry (too advanced for minute 1)
- Avoid discussing the content bridge (not relevant yet)
- Avoid pushing the personal story if the audience is not
  receptive (read the room)
- Avoid claiming it is proven (it is a suggestion)
- Avoid claiming it will solve everything (it solves ONE thing:
  the fact/fiction boundary)

**Ten minutes. One question. One demonstration. One solution.
One invitation. That is all.**
