# The Curriculum — Five Stages of Learning

## From "What is this?" to "I teach this to others."

---

## Overview

The curriculum mirrors the five stages in STAGING.md but
focuses on WHAT the learner practices at each stage, not
how the AI adapts (that is covered in STAGING.md).

```
Stage 1: SORT      → learn to put outputs in the right folder
Stage 2: PIPELINE  → learn to move findings through the bridge
Stage 3: DEBATE    → learn to hold competing paradigms
Stage 4: TRANSLATE → learn to distribute and communicate
Stage 5: TEACH     → learn to teach the framework itself
```

Each stage has:
- A skill to develop
- A practice exercise
- A completion criterion (how you know you are ready for next)
- An approximate duration (varies by individual)

---

## Stage 1 — SORT (1-2 weeks)

**The skill:** Distinguish between AI-generated fact and
AI-generated fiction by placing every output in the correct
domain folder.

**The practice:**
1. Create the five folders (VERIFIED, CREATIVE, FRONTIER,
   BRIDGE, ARCHIVE)
2. Work with AI on a topic you know well
3. After each AI response, decide: which folder does this
   belong in?
4. At the end of the session, review your sorting decisions
5. Were any CREATIVE outputs mistakenly placed in VERIFIED?
6. Were any VERIFIED outputs mistakenly placed in CREATIVE?

**Completion criterion:** The learner can sort 20 consecutive
AI outputs into the correct domain without error. The learner
can EXPLAIN why each output belongs where it was placed.

**Common mistakes at this stage:**
- Putting AI-generated hypotheses in VERIFIED/ because they
  sound authoritative
- Putting well-established facts in CREATIVE/ because the AI
  phrased them casually
- Not recognizing when the AI shifts domains mid-response
- Treating the AI's confidence level as a domain indicator
  (it is not — the AI sounds confident in ALL domains)

---

## Stage 2 — PIPELINE (2-4 weeks)

**The skill:** Move a finding from observation through
verification to confirmed status using the BRIDGE/ pipeline.

**The practice:**
1. Generate a hypothesis in CREATIVE/ using AI brainstorming
2. Identify what EVIDENCE would confirm or deny it
3. Place the hypothesis in BRIDGE/incoming
4. Gather evidence (literature review, data analysis, or
   experiment)
5. Compare evidence against the hypothesis in BRIDGE/matching
6. If the evidence supports: move to BRIDGE/confirmed
7. If the evidence contradicts: move to ARCHIVE/ with notes
8. If the evidence is mixed: move to FRONTIER/contested

**Completion criterion:** The learner has moved at least THREE
findings through the full pipeline — at least one confirmed,
at least one archived, at least one contested. The learner
can explain why each landed where it did.

**Common mistakes at this stage:**
- Skipping BRIDGE/matching and going straight to confirmed
  (the most dangerous shortcut)
- Treating a single supporting paper as "confirmed"
  (confirmation requires REPRODUCTION, not just citation)
- Abandoning contested findings instead of documenting the
  mixed evidence (the mixed evidence IS the science)
- Emotional attachment to a hypothesis that the evidence
  does not support (this is where the FRONTIER teaches humility)

---

## Stage 3 — DEBATE (1-3 months)

**The skill:** Hold two or more competing paradigms
simultaneously without prematurely selecting a winner.

**The practice:**
1. Identify a genuine scientific debate in your field
2. Place BOTH paradigms in FRONTIER/paradigms with equal
   standing
3. Document the evidence FOR each paradigm
4. Document the evidence AGAINST each paradigm
5. Identify what MEASUREMENT would settle the debate
6. Resist the urge to declare a winner before that measurement
   exists
7. Live with the uncertainty for at least one month

**Completion criterion:** The learner has maintained two
competing paradigms in FRONTIER/ for at least one month
without collapsing to a premature conclusion. The learner
can present BOTH paradigms to a colleague with equal
conviction.

**Common mistakes at this stage:**
- Unconsciously favoring one paradigm (confirmation bias)
- Presenting one paradigm as "mine" and the other as "theirs"
  (both are the field's — the FRONTIER belongs to science)
- Closing the debate prematurely because uncertainty is
  uncomfortable (uncertainty IS the science at this stage)
- Failing to document the AGAINST evidence for the favored
  paradigm (contradicting evidence is information, not noise)

**Why this stage takes the longest:** This is where the learner's
ego is challenged. Most researchers WANT to be right. The
FRONTIER demands they hold "I don't know yet" as a productive
state rather than a failure. This emotional skill takes longer
to develop than the technical ones.

---

## Stage 4 — TRANSLATE (Ongoing)

**The skill:** Convert verified findings into content that
different audiences can absorb — other scientists, the public,
students, policymakers.

**The practice:**
1. Take a finding from VERIFIED/
2. Write it for a peer in your field (full technical detail)
3. Write it for a scientist in a DIFFERENT field (cross-
   disciplinary translation)
4. Write it for a student (educational framing)
5. Write it for a non-scientist (public communication)
6. Compare the four versions — what changed? What was preserved?
   Where did you lose accuracy? Where did you gain accessibility?

**Completion criterion:** The learner has translated at least
ONE verified finding into four audience-specific versions AND
had each version reviewed by a member of that audience for
clarity and accuracy.

**Common mistakes at this stage:**
- Oversimplifying for the public (losing the truth to gain
  accessibility)
- Over-complicating for students (teaching at YOUR level, not
  theirs)
- Assuming another field uses the same vocabulary (they often
  do not — the same word means different things in different
  disciplines)
- Forgetting to label the source domain — every translation
  should carry: "Based on VERIFIED/ results from [study/data]"

---

## Stage 5 — TEACH (Mastery)

**The skill:** Teach the framework itself to another person.
Guide them through Stages 1-4. Improve the framework based on
what they struggle with.

**The practice:**
1. Find a student or colleague willing to try the framework
2. Walk them through the First Ten Minutes (FIRST_TEN_MINUTES.md)
3. Guide them through Stage 1 (SORT) — observe their mistakes
4. Adjust your teaching based on what confuses them
5. Document what worked and what did not
6. Propose improvements to the curriculum based on your
   teaching experience

**Completion criterion:** The learner has successfully guided
at least ONE person from Stage 1 to Stage 2 AND has documented
at least ONE improvement to the curriculum based on the
experience.

**Why teaching IS the final stage:** You do not fully understand
a framework until you teach it. The student's questions reveal
the gaps in your own understanding. The struggle to explain
reveals the assumptions you did not know you were making.
Teaching is the final verification — not of the student's
learning, but of YOUR understanding.

## The Timeline

| Stage | Duration | What Is Learned |
|-------|----------|----------------|
| 1. SORT | 1-2 weeks | The distinction between domains |
| 2. PIPELINE | 2-4 weeks | The verification process |
| 3. DEBATE | 1-3 months | The patience of uncertainty |
| 4. TRANSLATE | Ongoing | The skill of communication |
| 5. TEACH | Mastery | The depth of understanding |

**Total time to basic competency (Stages 1-2):** 3-6 weeks.
**Total time to research competency (Stages 1-3):** 2-4 months.
**Total time to full mastery (Stages 1-5):** 6-12 months.

These are ESTIMATES. Some people move faster. Some slower. The
criterion is DEMONSTRATED CAPABILITY, not elapsed time. A
person who can sort 20 outputs correctly after 3 days is ready
for Stage 2 regardless of the suggested timeline.
