# For Educators — Teaching Domain Separation to Students

## How to integrate the framework into existing courses.

---

## The Challenge

Students are already using AI. They are using it for essays,
for research, for problem sets, for brainstorming. Most are
using it WITHOUT any structural separation between what the
AI verified and what the AI generated. They cite AI outputs
as sources. They trust AI confidence as accuracy.

This is not the students' fault. No one taught them the
distinction. The AI does not teach it. The institutions have
not yet addressed it. The students are operating in the gap.

## The Opportunity

You do not need to ban AI from your classroom. You need to
teach students HOW to use it. The framework provides the
structure. You provide the guidance.

## Integration Methods

### Method 1 — The Five-Folder Assignment (Any Course)

**Week 1:** Students create five folders on their computer:
VERIFIED, CREATIVE, FRONTIER, BRIDGE, ARCHIVE.

**Week 2:** Students complete a research task using AI. For
EVERY AI output they use, they must place it in the correct
folder and write ONE SENTENCE explaining why it belongs there.

**Week 3:** In-class discussion: which outputs were hardest to
classify? Why? What made certain outputs look like VERIFIED
when they were actually CREATIVE?

**Assessment:** Not graded on whether the AI outputs were "good"
or "bad." Graded on whether the SORTING was accurate and the
reasoning was sound.

### Method 2 — The Pipeline Project (Research Courses)

**Assignment:** Students use AI to generate a hypothesis
(CREATIVE/), then move it through the full pipeline:

1. Generate hypothesis with AI brainstorming (CREATIVE/)
2. Identify evidence needed (BRIDGE/incoming)
3. Gather and compare evidence (BRIDGE/matching)
4. Reach a conclusion: confirmed, contested, or archived
5. Present the FULL CHAIN — not just the conclusion, but the
   journey through each domain

**Assessment:** Graded on the PROCESS, not just the conclusion.
A well-documented failure (hypothesis archived with clear
reasoning) scores as high as a confirmed finding.

### Method 3 — The Paradigm Debate (Advanced Courses)

**Assignment:** Students are given a scientific debate with
two competing paradigms. They must:

1. Document BOTH paradigms in FRONTIER/ with equal effort
2. Present evidence FOR each
3. Present evidence AGAINST each
4. Identify what measurement would resolve the debate
5. Present BOTH to the class WITHOUT declaring a winner

**Assessment:** Graded on the BALANCE of presentation. If the
student clearly favored one paradigm, they must explain why
their objectivity drifted and what they would do differently.

### Method 4 — The Translation Exercise (Communication Courses)

**Assignment:** Take one VERIFIED finding and write it for
four audiences:

1. A peer scientist in the same field
2. A scientist in a different field
3. A student two years behind them
4. A family member with no science background

**Assessment:** Each version is reviewed by a MEMBER of that
audience (peer reviews peer version, family member reviews
family version). Graded on whether the science survived the
translation AND whether the audience understood it.

## Common Student Mistakes (And How to Address Them)

| Mistake | Why It Happens | How To Address |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| Citing AI output as a source | The AI sounds authoritative | Show them the demonstration from FIRST_TEN_MINUTES: the AI has equal confidence in fact and fiction |
| Putting everything in VERIFIED/ | They want to feel certain | Ask them: "What evidence would DISPROVE this? If you cannot answer, it is not verified." |
| Avoiding CREATIVE/ | They feel speculation is "wrong" | Explain: CREATIVE/ is where EVERY discovery starts. Einstein's thought experiments were CREATIVE/ before they were VERIFIED/. |
| Skipping BRIDGE/ | They want to go straight to conclusions | Show them a historical example where skipping verification produced a retracted paper. The pipeline exists to protect their reputation. |
| Discomfort with FRONTIER/ | Uncertainty feels like failure | Normalize uncertainty: "The greatest scientists in history spent YEARS in the FRONTIER before the measurement resolved. You are in good company." |

## The Grading Philosophy

The framework suggests a shift in what is assessed:

| Traditional Assessment | Framework Assessment |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| "Is the answer correct?" | "Is the answer in the correct DOMAIN?" |
| "Did the student find the right source?" | "Does the student know what KIND of source they found?" |
| "Is the paper well-argued?" | "Is the evidence chain from CREATIVE → BRIDGE → VERIFIED visible?" |
| "Did the student use AI appropriately?" | "Can the student DISTINGUISH what the AI contributed from what was independently verified?" |

The framework does not replace scientific rigor. It makes
scientific rigor VISIBLE — to the student, to the educator,
and to the AI.

## Institutional Adoption Path

If this works in your classroom:

1. Document the results (student performance, sorting accuracy)
2. Share with colleagues in your department
3. Propose a pilot across multiple courses
4. Measure: does the framework improve research integrity in
   student work?
5. Publish the findings (through the BRIDGE/ pipeline,
   naturally)

The framework does not require institutional mandate to start.
One classroom. One educator. One semester. If the results are
positive, the evidence speaks for itself.
