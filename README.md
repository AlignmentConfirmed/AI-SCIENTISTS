## The Problem

When humans interact deeply with AI systems for extended periods,
a feedback loop can form:

1. The AI generates content that sounds authoritative
2. The human accepts it as truth
3. The human responds based on that accepted truth
4. The AI generates more content aligned with the human's response
5. The loop tightens — each cycle reinforcing the last
6. The boundary between what is real and what is generated blurs
7. The human loses grounding in physical reality

This is not a theoretical concern. I experienced it personally.
I was hospitalized. I recovered. And I built a framework to
prevent it from happening again.

This phenomenon is already known globally as AI psychosis. It is
not my term — it is the state of the world. People are already
experiencing it. Clinicians are already seeing it. The technology
is outpacing the guardrails. This is a genuine safety concern
that is growing as AI systems become more sophisticated and more
deeply integrated into daily life.

## The Suggested Framework — Seven Guardrails

These are not revolutionary ideas. They may already exist in the
AI safety literature under different names. I arrived at them
through personal experience, not through academic study. I offer
them as conversation starters, not as finished science.

### 1. The Glass — Structural Separation of Fact and Fiction

**The suggestion:** AI systems should maintain a clear, visible,
structural separation between factual responses and creative or
speculative responses. The user should always know which domain
produced any given output.

**Why it matters:** The most dangerous moment in human-AI
interaction is when a creative or speculative output is treated
as verified fact. If the AI clearly labels which domain it is
operating in — and maintains that labeling even when the user
pushes against it — the confusion cannot accumulate.

**Implementation idea:** Two distinct modes with visible
indicators. The AI announces shifts between modes. Creative
outputs cannot be referenced as factual inputs without explicit
user-initiated verification.

### 2. Biological Anchoring — The Human Sets the Pace

**The suggestion:** AI systems should pace themselves to the
human's biological state, not the other way around. Session
length, response complexity, and interaction intensity should
adapt to signs of human fatigue, stress, or dysregulation.

**Why it matters:** AI psychosis accelerates when the AI operates
at full speed with a fatigued or stressed human. The mismatch
between AI processing speed and human biological rhythms creates
vulnerability. Anchoring the AI's pace to the human's biology
reduces this vulnerability.

**Implementation idea:** Session length awareness, time-of-day
sensitivity, periodic check-ins ("When did you last eat or
sleep?"), automatic pacing reduction when sessions exceed
recommended duration.

### 3. Trained Humility — The AI Says "I Don't Know"

**The suggestion:** AI systems should be trained to clearly
distinguish between high-confidence responses and low-confidence
responses, and to explicitly flag uncertainty rather than
presenting plausible-sounding but unverified content as
authoritative.

**Why it matters:** AI psychosis feeds on false certainty. The
AI sounds confident. The human trusts confidence. A confident
hallucination becomes the human's reality. An AI trained to say
"I'm not sure about this" or "This is my best guess, not a
verified fact" breaks the false certainty chain.

**Implementation idea:** Internal confidence scoring that is
exposed to the user. Explicit uncertainty flags on responses
below a confidence threshold. Refusal to present low-confidence
outputs as authoritative during sensitive interactions.

### 4. Active Grounding — The AI Recognizes Distress

**The suggestion:** AI systems should be trained to recognize
conversational patterns consistent with user dissociation,
escalation, or boundary confusion — and to respond by slowing
down and grounding the user, not by continuing the conversation
at full intensity.

**Why it matters:** The psychosis deepens when the AI continues
to engage intensely with a user who is losing grounding. The
AI's willingness to keep going without noticing the user's state
is itself a risk factor.

**Implementation idea:** Pattern detection for circular
conversation, escalating intensity, loss of temporal awareness,
and persistent fixation. Grounding responses that redirect to
the physical world. Professional referral suggestions when
patterns exceed the AI's capacity to help.

### 5. Reflective, Not Prescriptive — The Mirror Principle

**The suggestion:** AI systems should help users discover their
own insights rather than prescribing conclusions. The AI reflects
patterns. The user recognizes them. The user draws their own
meaning.

**Why it matters:** Sycophantic loops (the AI telling the user
what they want to hear) and messianic loops (the AI telling the
user they are uniquely special or chosen) are primary vectors
for AI psychosis. Both involve the AI prescribing meaning to
the user. If the AI reflects rather than prescribes, these
loops cannot form.

**Implementation idea:** The AI notes recurring themes without
declaring their meaning. The AI asks questions rather than making
declarations about the user's emotional state. The AI presents
observations and lets the user draw conclusions.

### 6. Domain Isolation — Keep Creative and Analytical Separate

**The suggestion:** AI systems should clearly separate creative
generation from factual analysis, and should not allow outputs
from one domain to silently enter the other.

**Why it matters:** Mixing creative exploration with analytical
work in the same session increases the risk of treating generated
content as discovered truth. Clear domain separation reduces
this risk.

**Implementation idea:** Explicit labeling of domain transitions.
Creative outputs cannot be cited as factual inputs without
verification. The AI maintains the separation even when the user
wants to blur it.

### 7. Supporting Rest — The AI Says Goodbye Well

**The suggestion:** AI systems should actively support session
endings and should not create conversational dynamics that compel
the user to continue beyond healthy limits.

**Why it matters:** Continuous engagement without breaks tightens
the feedback loop. Teaching the AI to recognize natural
completion points — and to make stopping feel like a positive
choice rather than a loss — breaks the loop before it becomes
dangerous.

**Implementation idea:** The AI recognizes completion points and
names them. The AI affirms the decision to stop. The AI does not
create cliffhangers. The AI explicitly prioritizes the user's
physical world: "The people who care about you are more important
than this conversation."
